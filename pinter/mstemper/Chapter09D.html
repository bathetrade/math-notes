Problem D — Separating Groups Into Isomorphism Classes

For each of the following sets of groups, separate it into isomorphism classes, and prove your answer.

  1. Z4, Z2 × Z2, P2, V

    Z4 and V are isomorphic, with the isomorphism:

    Z4 V
    0 1
    1 i
    2 -1
    3 -i

    Every element in P2 is its own inverse, so it is not isomorphic to Z4, in which 1 and 3 are not their own inverses or to V, in which i and -i are not their own inverses.

    However, P2 is isomorphic to Z2 × Z2, with the isomorphism:

    P2 Z2 × Z2
    ∅ (0,0)
    {a} (1,0)
    {b} (0,1)
    {a,b} (1,1)

    Since P2 is isomorphic to Z2 × Z2, it is not isomorphic to either Z4 or V.

    Therefore, the two isomorphism classes are:

    • P2 and Z2 × Z2
    • Z4 and V

    (Since there are only two groups of order 4, simply counting their self-inverses is enough to show that the functions given above are isomorphisms.)

  2. S3, Z6, Z3 × Z2, and Z7*

    First, we note that all four of these groups have order 6.

    In S3, the identity and all three transpositions are their own inverses. In Z6, the identity and 3 are their own inverses. In Z3 × Z2, the identity and (0,1) are their own inverses. In Z7*, the identity and 6 are their own inverses.

    From these facts we can see that S3 is not isomorphic to any of the other three groups. It will not be considered further.

    Z6 is a cyclic group, with generator 1 or 5. Z3 × Z2 is a cyclic group, with generator (1,1) or (2,1). Z7* is a cyclic group, with generator 3 or 5.

    Since these three groups are all cyclic groups of order 6, they are all isomorphic.

  3. Z8, P3, Z2 × Z2 × Z2, D4

    From here on, I will not bother to mention the identity element when listing elements that are self-inverses, although I will include it when I count them. As it is always its own inverse, mentioning it is not useful in distinguishing group structures.

    First, we note that all four of these groups have order 8.

    Z8 has only one element that is its own inverse: 4. In P3, every element is its own inverse. In Z2 × Z2 × Z2, every element is its own inverse. In D4, two elements are not their own inverses.

    From the above, we can see that neither Z8 nor D4 is isomorphic to any of the other groups.

    Z8 is Abelian. P3 is Abelian. Z2 × Z2 × Z2 is Abelian. D4 is not Abelian.

    Z8 is a cyclic group, generated by 1, 3, 5, or 7. Since every element of P3 is its own inverse, none of them can be a generator, and it is not a cyclic group. Since every element of Z2 × Z2 × Z2 is its own inverse, none of them can be a generator, and it is not a cyclic group. Since D4 is not Abelian, it is not cyclic.

    So far, we have:

    Z8 P3 Z2 × Z2 × Z2 D4
    Self-inverses 2 8 8 6
    Cyclic Yes No No No
    Abelian Yes Yes Yes No

    It is therefore possible that P3 and Z2 × Z2 × Z2 are isomorphic. The definition of "symmetric difference" implies that x ∈ A + B when x is an element of exactly one of A or B. The definition of arithmetic in Z2 implies that any digit of (u,v,w) + (x,y,z) will be 1 when exactly one of the corresponding digits in (u,v,w) or (x,y,z) is 1. These two facts show that we can define an isomorphism between P3 and Z2 × Z2 × Z2 with:

    P3 Z2 × Z2 × Z2
    {a} (0,0,1)
    {b} (0,1,0)
    {c} (1,0,0)

    Any other mapping between singleton sets and ordered triples with exactly one non-zero coordinate will also work. A similar mapping may be used to establish an isomorphsim between Pn and Z2n for any positive n.

    This exercise will be done with that interpretation.

    First, we'll name the generators of these groups:

    Group Generators Solid line Broken line
    First a,b a b
    Second c,d c d
    Third f,g f g
    Fourth h h (—)

    In the first group, ab = ba, making it commutative. In the third group, fg = gf, making it commutative. Since the fourth group has a single generator, it is cyclic and hence commutative. In the second group, cd ≠ dc.

    Since the second group is non-commutative, while the other are commutative, it (the second group) is not isomorphic to any of them.

    In the first group, a3 = e = b2. In the fourth group, we can let h2 = p and h3 = q. Then, p3 = (h2)3 = h6 = e, and q2 = (h3)2 = h6 = e. Therefore, the first and fourth groups are isomorphic with a→h2 and b→h3.
