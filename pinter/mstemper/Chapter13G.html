Problem G — Survey of all Ten-Element Groups

Let G be any group of order ten.

  1. Reason as in Problem F to show that G = {e, a, b, ab, b2, ab2, b3, ab3, b4, ab4} where ord(a) = 2 and ord(b) = 5.

    As shown in Chapter 10, Problem E3, since 2 and 5 are relatively prime, the products aibj (0≤i<2, 0≤j<5) are all distinct.

  2. Prove that ba ∉ {e, a, b, b2, b3, b4}.

    ba ≠ e If ba=e, then a = b-1. However, the order of an element is the same as the order of its inverse. Since ord(a) = 2 and ord(b) = 5, this cannot be true.

    ba ≠ a If ba=a, then b=e. Since ord(b) = 5 and ord(e) = 1, this cannot be true.

    ba ≠ b If ba=b, then a=e. Since ord(a) = 2 and ord(e) = 1, this cannot be true.

    ba ≠ b2 If ba=b2, then a=b. By Chapter 10, Problem E3, this is not true.

    ba ≠ b3 If ba=b3, then a=b2. By Chapter 10, Problem E3, this is not true.

    ba ≠ b4 If ba=b4, then a=b3. By Chapter 10, Problem E3, this is not true.

    b3 = e is equivalent to ord(b) = 3. However, ord(b) was given as 5. Therefore, the assumption that ba = ab3 is false.
