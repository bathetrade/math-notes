Problem E — Conjugate Cycles

Prove each of the following in Sn:

  1. If α is any cycle and π any permutation, παπ-1 is called a conjugate of α.

    Let α = (a1 ... as) be a cycle and let π be a permutation in Sn. Then παπ-1 is the cycle (π(a1) ... π(as)).

    First, let's handle the case where π-1(b) ∉ {ai|1≤i≤s}. In this case, we have παπ-1(b) = ππ-1(b) = b. This means that παπ-1 does not change b.

    Now, we can look at the case where π-1(b) ∈ {ai|1≤i≤s}. First, we observe that since π-1 is a bijection, there are n elements in this case.

    Let f = παπ-1. Then, (... f(b), f(f(b)), ...) is part of a cyle in f. For some aj, f(b) = π(aj). Then:

    f(b) π(aj) Given
    f(f(b)) f(π(aj)) Apply f to both sides
    f∘π(aj) Definition of composition of functions
      π∘α∘π-1∘π(aj) Definition of f
      π∘α(aj) Inverses

    Since (... aj, α(aj), ...) was any arbitrary part of the cycle α, and (... π(aj), πα(aj), ...) is part of a cycle in παπ-1, the result is proved.

  2. Conclude from Part 1 that any two cycles of the same length are conjugates of each other.

    Let α = (a1, ..., ak) and β = (b1, ..., bk) be two cycles in Sn, both of length k.

    Define θ(ai) = bi (1≤i≤k). Then, we may rewrite β as (θ(b1), ..., θ(bk)). Part 1 shows that β = θαθ-1, which means that β is a conjugate of α.

    Since our choice of which function to label "α" and which to label "β" was arbitrary, this shows that each is a conjugate of the other.

  3. If α and β are disjoint cycles, then παπ-1 and πβπ-1 are disjoint cycles.

    If the elements of α are labeled as "ai", then by Part 1, the elements of παπ-1 are π(ai). Labeling the elements of β as "bj" gives the elements of πβπ-1 as π(bj).

    Since α and β are disjoint, ai ≠ bj for all i,j. Since π is a permutation, it is one-to-one, which means that for all i,j π(ai) ≠ π(bj). This implies that παπ-1 and πβπ-1 are disjoint cycles.

  4. Let σ be a product α1 ... αt of t disjoint cycles of lengths l1 ... lt, respectively. Then πσπ-1 is also a product of t disjoint cycles of lengths l1 ... lt.

    The first step will be to prove a lemma, stating that πα1 ... αtπ-1 = πα1π-1 ... παtπ-1. The proof will be by induction on t.

    Base For any α1 and α2, we have:

    πα1α2π-1 πα1eα2π-1 Identity
    πα1π-1πα2π-1 Inverses

    Hypothesis Assume that πα1 ... αtπ-1 = πα1π-1 ... παtπ-1 for t=k.

    Step

    π(α1 ... αk)αk+1π-1 π(α1 ... αk)eαk+1π-1 Identity
    π(α1 ... αk)π-1παk+1π-1 Inverses
    (πα1π-1) ... (παkπ-1)(παk+1π-1) By induction hypothesis

    Since the lemma is true for two cycles, and its truth for k cycles implies its truth for k+1 cycles, it is true for any finite number of cycles greater than one.

    By Exercise 1, if αp is a cycle of length lp, παpπ-1 is also a cycle of length lp.

    Since αi and αj are disjoint when i≠j, παiπ-1 and παjπ-1 are, by Exercise 3. By the lemma, πσπ-1 is therefore the product of t disjoint cycles of lengths l1 ... lt.

  5. Let α1 and α2 be cycles of the same length and let β1 and β2 be cycles of the same length. Let α1 and β1 be disjoint and let α2 and β2 be disjoint. There is a permutation π such that α1β1 = πα2β2π-1.

    We can rewrite πα2β2π-1 as πα2π-1πβ2π-1. Since α1 and α2 have the same lengths, there is at least one permutation that, when conjugated with α2 gives α1. A similar statement is true for the βs.

    If we can find a single permutation π such that both πα2π-1 = α1 and πβ2π-1 = β1, we are done.

    We can let πα be a permutation such that παα2πα-1 = α1, and any element that πα-1 does not map into α2 is mapped into itself. Similarly, let πβ be a permutation such that πββ2πβ-1 = β1, and any element that πβ-1 does not map into β2 is mapped into itself.

    As shown in Exercise 1, when a permutation is conjugated with a cycle, only the elements that the inverse of the permutation map into the cycle are changed by the conjugate. Since α2 and β2 are disjoint cycles, πα does not modify any elements that are changed by πβ and vice-versa. Therefore, π = πα∘πβ has the desired properties.
