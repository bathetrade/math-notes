Problem I — Group Automorphisms

An automorphism is an isomorphism from a group to itself.

  1. Verify that

    f = 
    0 1 2 3 4 5
    0 5 4 3 2 1

    is an automorphism of Z6.

    Since Z6 is an Abelian group and f(x) = x-1, Exercise 3 of Problem H shows that this is an automorphism.

  2. Verify that

    f1 = 
    0 1 2 3 4
    0 2 4 1 3
    f2 = 
    0 1 2 3 4
    0 3 1 4 2
    f3 = 
    0 1 2 3 4
    0 4 3 2 1

    are automorphisms of Z5.

    f1 may also be expressed as f1(x) = x+x. Moving outside the system, this may be called "2x". Since 2 does not divide 5, this is a bijection, as may be seen from inspection of the tabular description.

    f1(p+q) = (p+q)+(p+q) = (p+p)+(q+q) = f(p)+f(q), so f1 preserves products. Since it's also a bijection, it is an automorphism.

    f2 may also be expressed as f2(x) = x+x+x. Moving outside the system, this may be called "3x". Since 3 does not divide 5, this is a bijection, as may be seen from inspection of the tabular description.

    f2(p+q) = (p+q)+(p+q)+(p+q) = (p+p+p)+(q+q+q) = f(p)+f(q), so f2 preserves products. Since it's also a bijection, it is an automorphism.

    f3 may also be expressed as f3(x) = -x. Since Z5 is Abelian, Exercise 3 of Problem H shows that it is an automorphism.

  3. If G is any group, and a is any element of G, prove that f(x) = axa-1 is an automorphism of G.

    This is a generalization of what was proved for conjugate cycles in Chapter 8, Problem E.

    First, we will prove that f is one-to-one:

    f(x) f(y) assumption
    axa-1 aya-1 definition of f
    axa-1a aya-1a post-multiply by a
    axe aye inverse
    ax ay identity
    a-1ax a-1ay pre-multiply by a-1
    ex ey inverse
    x y identity

    Since f(x)=f(y) implies x=y, f is one-to-one. Next, we will prove that f is onto G:

    Let p∈G. Since G is a group, it is closed, and (a-1pa)∈G. f(a-1pa) = a(a-1pa)a-1 = p. Therefore, f is onto G, and a bijection.

    Let q,r∈G. f(qr) = aqra-1 = aqera-1 = (aqa-1)(ara-1) = f(q)f(r). Therefore, f preserves products.

    Since f is a bijection from G to itself that preserves products, it is an automorphism of G.

  4. Since an automorphism on G is a bijection from G to G, it is a permutation of G. Prove that the set Aut(G) of all automorphisms of G is a subgroup of SG.

    For a subset of a group to be a (sub-)group, it must be closed under the group operation (which is in this case is composition of functions) and under inversion.

    I'm proving the more general case of isomorphisms, since it looks like it will be a useful result.

    First we prove that the composite of two isomorphisms is an isomorphism. Let f:A → B and g:B → C be isomorphisms.

    Since they are isomorphisms, f and g are bijections. By their definitions, Range(f) = Domain(g). It is a basic result of set theory that if f and g are bijections and Range(f) = Domain(g), g∘f is a bijection.

    Since f is an isomorphism, it preserves products, and f(pq) = f(p)f(q). By the definition of f, f(p) and f(q) are in the domain of g. Since g is an isomorphism, it preserves products, and g(f(p)f(q)) = [g∘f](p)[g∘f](q). Combining these, we see that [g∘f](pq) = g(f(p)f(q)) = [g∘f](p)[g∘f](q). Therefore, g∘f preserves products.

    Since g∘f is a bijection that preserves products, it is an isomorphism.

    In the case that A = B = C, g∘f is an automorphism on A. Therefore, Aut(G) is closed under the group operation of SG.

    Again, I'm proving the more general result because it looks like it will be useful in the future.

    Next, we prove that the inverse of an isomorphism is an isomorphism.

    Let p:U → V be an isomorphism. Since p is an isomorphism, it is a bijection. As shown in Chapter 6, this implies that p-1:V → U exists and is a bijection.

    Since p is an isomorphism, it preserves products, giving p(xy) = p(x)p(y). Applying p-1 gives us p-1(p(xy)) = p-1(p(x)p(y)). Since p-1(p(xy)) = xy = p-1(p(x))p-1(p(y)), this gives us p-1(p(x)p(y)) = p-1(p(x))p-1(p(y)), showing that p-1 preserves products.

    Since p-1 is a bijection that preserves products, it is an isomporphism.

    In the case where U = V, p is then an automorphism on U. Therefore, Aut(G) is closed under inversion.

    Since Aut(G) is a subset of SG that is closed under the group operation and inversion, Aut(G) is a subgroup of SG.
