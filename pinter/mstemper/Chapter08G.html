Problem G — Even/Odd Permutations in Subgroups of Sn

Prove or disprove each of the following in Sn:

  1. Let α1, ..., αr be distinct even permutations, and β an odd permutation. Then α1β, ... αrβ are distinct odd permutations.

    As shown in Problem C, Exercise 2c, the product of an even permutation and an odd permutation is odd. Therefore, α1β, ... αrβ are odd permutations.

    To show that α1β, ... αrβ are distinct, contradiction will be used.

    Assume that for some i≠j (1≤i≤r and 1≤j≤r), αiβ = αjβ. Then, αiββ-1 = αjββ-1, which gives αie = αje, or αi = αj, which contradicts the fact that the αs are distinct.

  2. If β1, ..., βr are distinct odd permutations, β1β1, β1β2, ... β1βr are distinct even permutations.

    As shown in Problem C, Exercise 2b, the product of two odd permutations is even. Therefore, β1β1, β1β2, ... β1βr are even permutations.

    To show that β1β1, β1β2, ... β1βr are distinct, contradiction will again be used.

    Assume that for some i≠j (1≤i≤r and 1≤j≤r), β1βi = β1βj. Then, β1-1β1βi = β1-1β1βj. Then, eβi = eβj and βi = βj, which contradicts the fact that the βs are distinct.

    In Sn, there are the same number of odd permutations as even permutations.

    Assume n≥2. Label two of the elements of the underlying set "μ" and "η". Then Sn contains the transposition (μη), which is odd. Therefore, if n≥2, Sn contains at least one odd permutation.

    With n≥2, let "r" be the number of distinct even permutations in Sn. Since Sn contains an odd permutation, we can take the product of each of the r distinct even permutations with this odd permutation. Each of these products is a distinct odd permutation, which means that Sn must contain at least r odd permutations.

    With n≥2, let "s" be the number of distinct odd permutations in Sn. (Note that s≥1.) Select any one of them and form its product with each of the odd permutations (including itself). This will yield s distinct even permutations, which means that the number of distinct even permutations in Sn is at least s.

    Since the number of odd permutations in Sn must be at least as large as the number of even permutations and the number of even permutations must be at least as large as the number of odd permutations, Sn must have as many odd permutations as even permutations.

  4. This is denoted by An, and called the alternating group on n symbols.

    The set of all even permutations is a subgroup of Sn.

    Let α and β be two even permutations in Sn (not necessarily unique). As shown in Problem C, Exercise 2a, the product of two even permutations is even. Therefore, the set of all even permutations in Sn is closed under multiplication.

    It was shown in the text that the identity permutation is even. Since the product of an even permutation and an odd permutation is odd, and the product of a permutation and its inverse is even, the inverse of an even permutation must also be even. Therefore, the set of all even permutations in Sn is closed under inversion.

    Since the set of all even permutations in Sn is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of Sn.

  5. Let H be any subgroup of Sn. H either contains only even permutations or it contains equal numbers of even and odd permutations.

    Since H is a group, it is closed under multiplication. As shown in Exercise 2, it must have at least as many even permutations as odd ones.

    Let H contain at least one odd permutation. Since it is a group, it contains the identity, which is even. Since it is a group and therefore closed under multiplication, it must have at least as many odd permutations as even ones, as shown in Exercise 1.

    It is therefore possible for H to either contain only even permutations or for it to contain equal numbers of even and odd permutations.
