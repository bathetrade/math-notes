Problem E — Counting Elements and Their Inverses

Let G be a group and let S = {x∈G : x≠x-1}. Prove the following:

  1. The number of elements of a finite group G that are not their own inverse is even.

    Choose an element of G that is not its own inverse. Call it "a". Let b≠a be a-1. Then, since the inverse of the inverse of an element is the element itself, b-1=a.

    If G has another element, c, that is not its own inverse, d=c-1∉{a,b,c}. Therefore, we have added two elements to S.

    Since elements come into S in pairs, |S| must be even.

  2. Since we're discussing whether sets are even or odd, it is safe to assume that these are all finite sets.

    |G-S| is even or odd, depending upon whether |G| is even or odd.

    Since S⊂G, |G-S| = |G|-|S|.

    Since |S| is even, |G|-|S| will be even or odd, depending upon whether |G| is even or odd.

  3. If |G| is even, ∃a∈G such that a≠e and a=a-1.

    Since |S| is even, |G-S| is even. Since e=e-1, e∉S, and |(G-S)-{e}| is odd. Therefore, |(G-S)-{e}| has at least one element.

  4. With G a finite Abelian group, G = {e, a1, a2, ... an}, prove (a1a2...an)2 = e.

    First, we will split G-{e} into two subsets, S, which contains all of the elements of G that are not their own inverses, and T = (G-{e})-S, which contains all of the elements (other than the identity) that are their own inverses.

    Redo 4-6 using induction on the sizes of S and T.

    Since G is an Abelian group, we can take the products of these sets independently and then multiply those products.

    Every element of S is paired with its inverse, so the product of the elements of S is e. This means that the product of all of the non-identity elements in G is the same as the product of all of the elements of T. In turn, (G-{e})2 = T2.

    Again, we can arbitrarily reorder the elements of T2 so that each element is multiplied by itself — which is its inverse. This reduces the product to 3.

  5. With G a finite Abelian group G = {e, a1, a2, ... an}, and containing no elements (other than the identity) that are their own inverses, prove (a1a2...an) = e.

    Since each element of G (other than the identity element) is paired with its inverse, the product of its elements is e.

  6. With G a finite Abelian group G = {e, a1, a2, ... an}, and containing exactly one element, x, (other than the identity) that is its own inverse, prove (a1a2...an) = x.

    Examining G-{e,x}, we see that every element of it is paired with its inverse. Therefore, the product of the elements of G-{e,x} is e. Multiplying that by x gives x.
