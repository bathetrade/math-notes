Problem C — Groups of Permutations of R

A is a subset of R, and G is a set of permutations of A. Show that G is a subgroup of SA and give its table.

  1. A = R-{0,1}. G = {e,f,g}, where f(x)=1/(1-x) and g(x)=(x-1)/x.

    f(a) = 1/(1-a) and f(b) = 1/(1-b), when a≠1≠b. Setting f(a)=f(b) gives 1/(1-a) = 1/(1-b). Given the restriction on the domain of f, we can multiply both sides by (1-a)(1-b), yielding 1-b = 1-a, or a = b. Therefore, f is one-to-one. Since 0 has no reciprocal, it is not in the range of f. Let y = 1/(1-x). Then, y=1 when x=0. Since 0 is excluded from the range of f, 1 is not in the range of f. For any p∈R-{0,1}, we have p = 1/(1-x). Solving for x gives x = (p-1)/p, with p∉{0,1}. Therefore, f is onto R-{0,1}, and an element of SA.

    g(a) = (a-1)/a and g(b) = (b-1)/b, when a≠0≠b. Setting g(a)=g(b) gives (a-1)/a = (b-1)/b. Given the restriction on the domain of g, we can multiply both sides by ab, yielding ab-b = ab-a, or a = b. Therefore, g is one-to-one. Let y = (x-1)/x. Then y=0 when x=1. Since 1 is excluded from the range of g and g is one-to-one, 0 is not in the range of g. Setting y = 1 = (x-1)/x gives x-1 = x, so 1 is not in the range of g. For any q∈R-{0,1}, we have q = (x-1)/x. Solving for x gives x = 1/(1-q), with q∉{0,1}. Therefore, g is onto R-{0,1}, and an element of SA.

    Since the identity function, e, on any set is a permutation on that set, we have {e,f,g}⊂SA.

    [g∘f](x) = ((1/(1-x)-1)/(1/(1-x)) = (1-x)(1/(1-x)-1) = 1-(1-x) = x, so g = f-1. [f∘g](x) = 1/(1-(x-1)/x) = x/(x-x-1) = x, so f = g-1. Therefore, G is closed under inversion.

    [f∘f](x) = 1/(1-1/(1-x)) = (1-x)/((1-x)-1) = (1-x)/(-x) = (x-1)/x = g(x). [g∘g](x) = (((x-1)/x)-1)/((x-1)/x) = (x-1-x)/(x-1) = -1/(x-1) = 1/(x-1) = f(x). With this and the previous result we have shown that, G is closed under multiplication.

    Therefore, G is a subgroup of SA. Its table follows:

    ∘ e f g
    e e f g
    f f g e
    g g e f

  2. A = R-{0}. G = {e,f,g,h}, where f(x)=1/x, g(x)=-x, and h(x)=-1/x.

    f(a) = 1/a and f(b) = 1/b, when ab≠0. Setting f(a)=f(b) gives 1/a = 1/b, or a=b. Therefore, f is one-to-one. Since any non-zero real has a reciprocal, f is onto R-{0}. Therefore, f is a permutation on R-{0}, and an element of SA.

    g(x) is obviously one-to-one and onto R-{0}, so it is also a permutation and an element of SA.

    The demonstration that h is an element of SA is nearly identical to that for f. We therefore have {e,f,g,h}⊂SA.

    [f∘f](x) = x = e(x), so f is its own inverse.

    [g∘g](x) = x = e(x), so g is its own inverse.

    [h∘h](x) = x = e(x), so h is its own inverse.

    Therefore, {e,f,g,h} is closed under inversion.

    [f∘g](x) = 1/-x = h(x). [f∘h](x) = -x = g(x).

    [g∘f](x) = 1/-x = h(x). [g∘h](x) = 1/x = f(x).

    [h∘f](x) = -x = g(x). [h∘g](x) = 1/x = f(x).

    This shows that G is closed under multiplication. Therefore, G is a subgroup of SA. Its table follows:

    ∘ e f g h
    e e f g h
    f f e h g
    g g h e f
    h h g f e

  3. A = R-{0,1}. G = {e,f,g,h,j,k}, where f(x)=1-x, g(x)=1/x, h(x)=1/(1-x), j(x)=(x-1)/x, k(x)=x/(x-1).

    f(a) = 1-a and f(b) = 1-b. Setting f(a)=f(b) give a = b, so f is one-to-one. Since 1-x = 0 when x=1 and 1-x = 1 when x=0, neither 0 nor 1 is in the range of f. For any p∈R-{0,1}, f(1-p) = p, so f is onto R-{0,1}. Therefore, f is a permutation on R-{0,1} and an element of SA.

    g(a) = 1/a and g(b) = 1/b, so g(a) = g(b) only when a=b, and g is one-to-one. Since 1/x≠0 and 1/x=1 when x is outside the domain of g, neither 0 nor 1 is in the range of g. For any q∈R-{0,1}, g(1/q) = q, so g is onto R-{0,1}. Therefore, g is a permutation on R-{0,1} and an element of SA.

    The proofs that h and j are elements of SA was given in Exercise 1 of this problem.

    With a,b∉{0,1}, k(a) = a/(a-1) and k(b) = b/(b-1). Setting k(a)=k(b) gives a/(a-1) = b/(b-1). With the given restriction on the domain of k, this yields a = b, so k is one-to-one. 0/(0-1) = 0. Since 0 is not in the domain of k, it is not in its range, either. Since x≠x-1, 1 is not in the range of k. For any r∉{0,1}, let f(x)=r. Solving for x gives x = r/(r-1), so k is onto R-{0,1} and an element of SA.

    Therefore, {e,f,g,h,j,k}⊂SA.

    [f∘f](x) = 1-(1-x) = x = e(x), so f is its own inverse.

    [g∘g](x) = 1/(1/x) = x = e(x), so g is its own inverse.

    [h∘j](x) = 1/(1-(x-1)/x) = x = e(x), so h = j-1.

    [j∘h](x) = (1/(1-x)-1)/(1/(1-x)) = x = e(x), so j = h-1.

    [k∘k](x) = ((x/(x-1))/((x/(x-1))-1) = x = e(x), so k is its own inverse.

    Therefore, {e,f,g,h,j,k} is closed under inversion.

    The following table shows that {e,f,g,h,j,k} is closed under multiplication and hence a subgroup of SA:

    ∘ e f g h j k
    e e f g h j k
    f f e j k g h
    g g h e f k j
    h h g k j e f
    j j k f e h g
    k k j h g f e

  4. A = R-{0,1,2}. G is the subgroup of SA generated by f(x)=2-x and g(x)=2/x.

    It is obvious that f and g are one-to-one.

    Since 2-0 = 2, 2-1 = 1, and 2-2 = 0, the exclusion of these from the domain of f also excludes them from its range. For any a∈R-{0,1,2}, f(2-a) = a, so f is onto R-{0,1,2}, and hence an element of SA.

    Since 2/1 = 2, 2/2 = 1, and 0 has no reciprocal, the exclusion of {0,1,2} from the domain of g also excludes them from its range. For any b∈R-{0,1,2}, g(2a) = a, so g is onto R-{0,1,2}, and hence an element of SA.

    Since the composition of permutations is a permutation, all possible combinations of f and g will be elements of SA.

    f and g are their own inverses.

    Let h(x) = [f∘g](x) = 2-(2/x).

    Let j(x) = [g∘f](x) = 2/(2-x). (Note that since f∘g ≠ g∘f, this subgroup will not be commutative.)

    Let k(x) = [f∘j](x) = (2-2x)/(2-x).

    Let l(x) = [g∘j](x) = x/(x-1).

    Finally, let m(x) = [f∘l](x) = (x-2)/(x-1).

    The table follows:

    ∘ e f g h j k l m
    e e f g h j k l m
    f f e h g k j m l
    g g j e l f m h k
    h h k f m e l g j
    j j g l e m f k h
    k k h m f l e j g
    l l m j k g h e j
    m m l k j h g f e
