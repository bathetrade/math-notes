Problem H — Generators of An and Sn

  1. Prove that the set T of all the transpositions in Sn generates Sn.

    As shown in Theorem 1, "[e]very permutation is either the identity, a single cycle, or a product of disjoint cycles." If we can show that the transpositions in Sn generate all of these, we are done.

    Note that this is only true when n≥2. If n=1, then there are no transpositions in Sn, even though Sn contains one element (the identity) and is a group.

    The assumption that n≥2 will be used.

    Identity Let (ab) be any transposition in Sn. The product (ab)(ab) is the identity. Therefore, the identity can be generated.

    Single Cycle It was shown in the text that "[...] every cycle can be expressed as a product of one or more transpositions." Therefore, any single cycle can be generated.

    Product of Disjoint Cycles Let π be any permutation in Sn that is the product of disjoint cycles, say π = α1 ... αp. Since each αi can be expressed as a product of one or more transpositions, π can be expressed as the product of all of those products.

  2. Prove that the set T1 = {(12), (13), ..., (1n)} generates Sn.

    Let (xy) be any transposition in Sn such that (xy)∉ T1. (That is, x≠1≠y.)

    (xy) = (1x)(1y)(1x). Since {(1x), (1y)} ⊂ T1, (xy) can be generated from T1. Since the choice of x and y was arbitrary, this shows that any transposition not in T1 can be generated.

    Therefore, we have all of the transpositions, and Sn can be generated, as shown in Exercise 1.

  3. Prove that every even permutation is a product of one or more cycles of length 3. Conclude the set U of all cycles of length 3 generates An.

    Note that this is only true when n≥3. If n<2, there are no cycles of length 3. The assumption that n≥3 will be used.

    Let π be any even permutation. Express it as the product of transpositions. Since π is even, there is an even number of transpositions. Group them in pairs, starting from the left.

    For pair of transpositions, there are the following options: they are the same transposition, they are overlapping transpositions (having one element in common), or they are disjoint transpositions. Let's look at each case individually.

    Same transposition If an adjacent pair of transpositions has two instances of the same transposition, (xy)(xy), the product of this pair is the identity. (abc)(abc)(abc) = e, so (xy)(xy) may replaced (abc)(abc)(abc), which is the product of cycles of length three.

    Overlapping transpositions If an adjacent pair of transpositions has one element in common, (xz)(xy) = (zx)(xy) = (xz)(yx) = (zx)(yx) = (xyz), so this pair may be replaced by a cycle of length three.

    Disjoint transpositions If an adjacent pair of transpositions has no elements in common, (ab)(cd) = (cba)(acd), so this pair may be replaced by the product of cycles of length three.

    Since each pair of transpositions may be replaced by the product of one to three cycles of length three, π may be expressed as the product of one or more cycles of length three. Since π was any element of An may be expressed as the product of members of U, and U generates An.

  4. Prove that the set U1 = {(123), (124), ..., (12n)} generates An.

    As shown in the hint, a three-cycle that does not contain 1 or 2 may be expressed as (abc) = (1ca)(1ba).

    As shown in the hint, a three-cycle that starts with 1 may be expressed as (1ab) = (1b2)(12a)(12b). If a three-cycle has 1 in another position, it may be expressed as (ab1) = (b1a) = (1ab), and still expressed as (1b2)(12a)(12b).

    This allows us to replace the (1ca) or the (1ba) in the first part with the product of a cycle starting with 1 and ending with 2 and two elements of U1.

    A three-cycle that starts with 2 and does not contain 1 may be expressed as (2bc) = (1c2)(12b). Of course, (bc2) = (c2b) = (2bc). This again gives us the product of a cycle that starts with 1 and ends with 2 and an element of U1.

    As shown in the hint, a three-cycle that starts with 1 and ends with 2 may be expressed as (1b2) = (12b)2, which is the product of one (repeated) element of U1.

    The identity may be expressed as e = (12a)3.

    So, any three-cycle and the identity may be expressed as the product of one or more members of U1, which means that U1 generates U, which in turn generates An.

  5. Prove that the pair of cycles (12) and (12...n) generates Sn.

    As stated in the hint, (12...n)(12)(12...n)-1 = (23). Furthermore, (12...n)(i i+1)(12...n)-1 = (i+1 i+2) when i+1≤n and (i+1 1) when i+1=n. These are true because παπ-1 is the cycle (π(a1) ... π(as)), as shown in Exercise 1 of Problem E of this chapter.

    This shows that the cycles (12) and (12...n) generate {(12), (23), ..., (n-1 n), (n1)}.

    As stated in the hint, (12)(23)(12) = (13). More generally, (1p)(pq)(1p) = (1q), as may be seen either from direct multiplication or from realizing that (1p) = (1p)-1 and applying the result from Exercise 1 of Problem E.

    Between these two results, the cycles (12) and (12...n) generate the set of transpositions T1 = {(12), (13), ..., (1n)}. As shown in Exercise 2, T1 generates Sn.
