Problem E — General Properties of Equivalence Relations and Partitions

  1. Let {Ai : i ∈ I} be a partition of A. Let {Bj : j ∈ J} be a partition of B. Prove that {Ai × Bj : (i,j) ∈ I × J} is a partition of A × B.

    Let (a,b) ∈ A × B. Then a ∈ A and b ∈ B. Since {Ai : i ∈ I} is a partition of A, a ∈ exactly one member of {Ai : i ∈ I}. Call this element Ap. Similarly, b ∈ exactly one member of {Bj : j ∈ J}. Call this element Bq.

    Since a ∈ Ap and b ∈ Bq, (a,b) ∈ Ap × Bq. Therefore, every element of A × B ∈ some member of {Ai × Bj : (i,j) ∈ I × J}.

    Let (a,b) ∈ A × B. Assume that (a,b) ∈ Ap × Bq and that (a,b) ∈ Ar × Bs.

    Then, a ∈ Ap and a ∈ Ar. Since {Ai : i ∈ I} is a partition of A, a ∈ exactly one memmber of {Ai : i ∈ I}, which means that Ap = Ar. Similarly, it must be true that Bq = Bs.

    Therefore, Ap × Bq = Ar × Bs, and (a,b) ∈ exactly one member of {Ai × Bj : (i,j) ∈ I × J}.

    Therefore, {Ai × Bj : (i,j) ∈ I × J} is a partition of A × B.

  2. Let ~I be the equivalence relation corresponding to the above partition of A and let ~J be the equivalence relation corresponding to the above partition of B. Describe the equivalence relation corresponding to the above partition of A × B.

    Call the equivalence relation corresponding to the above partition of A × B "~I×J". Then, (w,x)~I×J(y,z) iff w~Iy and x~Jz.

  3. Let f:A → B be a function. Define ~ by a~b iff f(a) = f(b). Prove that ~ is an equivalence relation on A and describe its equivalence classes.

    Reflexive Since f(a) = f(a), a~a and this relation is reflexive.

    Symmetric If f(a) = f(b), then f(b) = f(a). Therefore, a~b ⇒ b~a, and this relation is symmetric.

    Transitive If a~b and b~c, then f(a) = f(b) and f(b) = f(c). Therefore, f(a) = f(c), giving a~c. Since a~b and b~c taken together imply a~c, this relation is transitive.

    Since this relation is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive, it is an equivalence relation.

    Let Ay = f-1(y). Then, the equivalence classes corresponding to this relation are {Ay : y ∈ B}.

  4. Let f:A → B be a function, and let {Bi : i ∈ I} be a partition of B. Prove that {f-1(Bi) : i ∈ I} is a partition of A. If ~I is the equivalence relation corresponding to the partition of B, describe the equivalence relation corresponding to the partition of A.

    Let x ∈ A. Then, f(x) ∈ B. Since {Bi : i ∈ I} is a partition of B, f(x) ∈ some member of {Bi : i ∈ I}. Call this member Bp. Since f(x) ∈ Bp, x ∈ f-1(Bp). Therefore every element of A ∈ some member of {f-1(Bi) : i ∈ I}.

    Let y ∈ f-1(Bp) and f-1(Bq). Then f(y) ∈ Bp and f(y) ∈ Bq. Since {Bi : i ∈ I} is a partition of B, it must be true that Bp = Bq, which means that f-1(Bp) = f-1(Bq). Therefore, y ∈ exactly one member of {f-1(Bi) : i ∈ I}, and it is a partition.

    The equivalence relation on A that corresponds to this partition is r~s iff f(r)~If(s).

    Symmetric Assume that a~3b. Then, a~1b and a~2b. Since ~1 and ~2 are equivalence relations, this implies that b~1a and b~2a, leading to b~3a, and ~3 is symmetric.

    Transitive Assume that a~3b and b~3c. Then, a~1b and b~1c. Since ~1 is an equivalence relation, it is transitive, leading to a~1c. Also, a~2b and b~2c. Since ~2 is an equivalence relation, it is transitive, leading to a~2c. Since a~1c and a~2c, a~3c, making ~3 transitive.
