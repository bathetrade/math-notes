Problem B — Examples of Orders of Elements

  1. I have a hunch that, in general, ord(a) in Zb is lcm(a,b)/a.

    What is the order of 10 in Z25?

    In Z25, 10+10 = 20, 10+20=5, 10+5=15, and 10+15=0, so the order of 10 is 5.

  2. What is the order of 6 in Z16?

    This does not contradict my hunch.

    In Z16, 6+6=12, 6+12=2, 6+2=8, 6+8=14, 6+14=4, 6+4=10, 6+10=0, so the order of 6 is 8.

  3. What is the order of

    a = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    6 1 3 2 5 4

    in S6?

    aa = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    4 6 3 1 5 2
    aaa = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    2 4 3 6 5 1
    aaaa = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    1 2 3 4 5 6

    I have a hunch that its order should be the same in Sn whenever n≥6.

    Its order is 4. This makes sense, since it can be expressed as a 4-cycle: (1642).

  4. What is the order of 1 in R* (the multiplicative group of non-zero reals)? What is the order of 1 in R (the additive group of reals)?

    In R*, 1 is the identity element, so its order is 1. In R, no matter how many times you add 1 to itself, the sum will never equal 0 (the identity element), so its order is infinite.

  5. If A = R - {0,1,2}, what is the order of f(x) = 2/(2-x) in SA?

    [f∘f](x) = (2-x)/(1-x); [f∘f∘f](x) = (2x-2)/x; [f∘f∘f∘f](x) = x. Therefore, ord(f) = 4.

  6. Can an element of an infinite group have finite order? Explain.

    Yes, it can. If the examples from exercises 4 and 5 in this problem weren't enough, how about -1 in <Q-{0}, *>? How about any solution to xn = 1 (for n any positive integer) in <C-{0}, *>?

  7. In Z24, list all elements ...

    1. ... of order 2:

      12

    2. ... of order 3:

      8, 16

    3. ... of order 4:

      6, 18

    4. ... of order 6:

      4
