Problem C — Even and Odd Permutations

  1. Determine which of the following permutations is even, and which is odd:

    1. π = 
      1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8
      7 4 1 5 6 2 3 8

      π = (173)(2456). By Exercise 4a, this is odd.

    2. (71864)

      (71864) = (46)(48)(41)(47), which is even.

    3. (12)(76)(345)

      (12)(76)(345) = (12)(76)(34)(45), which is even.

    4. (1276)(3241)(7812)

      (1276)(3241)(7812) = (1426)(378) = (62)(64)(61)(87)(83), which is odd.

    5. (123)(2345)(1357)

      (123)(2345)(1357) = (14752) = (27)(25)(24)(21), which is even.

  2. Prove each of the following:

    1. The product of two even permutations is even.

      Let π1 and π2 be two even permutations. Choose one way of writing each as the product of transpositions. Then, π1 has 2i transpositions and π2 has 2j transpositions. Their product has a total of 2(i+j) transpositions, and is therefore even.

    2. The product of two odd permutations is even.

      Let π1 and π2 be two odd permutations. Choose one way of writing each as the product of transpositions. Then, π1 has 2i+1 transpositions and π2 has 2j+1 transpositions. Their product has a total of 2i+1+2j+1 = 2(i+j+1) transpositions, and is therefore even.

    3. The product of an even permutation and an odd permutation is odd.

      Without loss of generality, let π1 be odd and let π2 be even. Choose one way of writing each as the product of transpositions. Then, π1 has 2i+1 transpositions and π2 has 2j transpositions. Their product has a total of 2i+1+2j = 2(i+j)+1 transpositions, and is therefore odd.

  3. Prove each of the following:

    1. A cycle of length l is even if l is odd.

    2. A cycle of length l is odd if l is even.

    Base The cycle (α0α1) has length two, which is even, and it can be written as one transposition, which makes it odd.

    Hypothesis Assume that, for any cycle of length l, if l is even, the cycle is odd, while if l is odd, the cycle is even.

    Step Let α = (α0α1...αl) be a cycle of length l+1. Then, define α- = (α1...αl) is a cycle of length l. By the Induction Hypothesis, if l is even, α- is odd, and if l is odd, α- is even.

    α = (α1α0)α-. Since α has one more transposition than α-, if α- is odd, α is even, while if α- is even, α is odd. At the same time, if l is even, l+1 is odd, while if l is odd, l+1 is even.

    In anticipation of the next exercise, these can be re-stated as: "A cycle of length l is even or odd depending on whether l-1 is even or odd."

  4. Prove each of the following:

    1. If α and β are cycles of length l and m, respectively, then αβ is even or odd depending on whether (l+m-2) is even or odd.

      This may be re-written as "αβ is even or odd depending on whether (l-1)+(m-1) is even or odd."

      By the previous exercise, a cycle is even or odd when its length less one is even or odd. By Exercise 2, the product of any two permutations (whether or not they are cycles) is odd if and only if exactly one of them is odd. By the laws of integer arithmetic, the sum of two integers (such as (l-1) and (m-1)) is odd if and only if exactly one of them is odd.

    2. If π = β1...βr, where each βi is a cycle of length li, then π is even or odd depending on whether l1 + ... + lr - r is even or odd.

      Re-write l1 + ... + lr -r as (l1-1) + ... (lr-1), and proceed by induction.

      Base The case for r=2 was proved in Exercise 3.

      By "parity", I mean "whether it is even or odd".

      Hypothesis Assume that the product β1...βk has the same parity as (l1-1) + ... + (lk-1).

      Step By Exercise 2, the parity of β1...βkβk+1 will differ from that of β1...βk only when βk+1 is odd. By the laws of integer arithmetic, the parity of (l1-1) + ... + (lk-1) + (lk+1-1) will differ from that of (l1-1) + ... + (lk-1) only when (lk+1-1) is odd.
