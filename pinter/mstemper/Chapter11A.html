Problem A — Examples of Cyclic Groups

  1. List the elements of <6> in Z16.

    6*1 6
    6*2 12
    6*3 2
    6*4 8
    6*5 14
    6*6 4
    6*7 10
    6*8 0

  2. List the elements of <f> in S6, where:

    f = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    6 1 3 2 5 4
    f1 = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    6 1 3 2 5 4
    f2 = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    4 6 3 1 5 2
    f3 = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    2 4 3 6 5 1
    f4 = 
    1 2 3 4 5 6
    1 2 3 4 5 6

  3. Describe the cyclic subgroup <1/2> in R*. Describe the cyclic subgroup <1/2> in R.

    In each case, <1/2> is identical to the subgroup generated by the group's inverse of 1/2.

    Since R* is multiplicative, <1/2> = {..., 16, 8, 4, 2, 1, 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, ...}.

    Since R is additive, <1/2> = {..., -2, -3/2, -1, -1/2, 0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, 2, ...}.

  4. If f(x) = x+1, describe the subgroup <f> of SR.

    <f> = {g(x) | g(x) = x+j ∧ j∈Z}.

  5. If f(x) = x+1, describe the subgroup <f> of F(R).

    Again, <f> = {g(x) | g(x) = x+j ∧ j∈Z}.

  6. Show that -1, as well as 1, is a generator of Z. Are there any other generators of Z? Explain! What are the generators of an arbitrary infinite cyclic group <a>?

    Let x be an element of Z that is generated by 1. Then, x = n*1 for some n. In that case, it is also true that x = (-n)*(-1). Therefore, every element of Z that is generated by 1 is also generated by -1. A similar argument shows that every element of Z that is generated by -1 is also generated by 1.

    There are no other generators of Z. It is obvious that 0 cannot be a generator, since n*0 = 0 for all n. If k>1 or k<-1 (k an integer), there is no n such that n*k = 1, so k cannot generate all of the elements of Z.

    The generators of an arbitrary infinite cyclic group <a> are a and a-1 for a group written multiplicatively. They are a and -a for a group written additively.

  7. Is R* cyclic?

    No. Let a be any element of R*. There is no integer p such that ap=-a so a cannot generate R*.

    Moreover, not even R+ is cyclic.

    Let a be any element of R+. If a≠1, there is no integer q such that aq = √a. If a=1, there is no integer q such that aq = 2.
