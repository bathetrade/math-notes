Problem D — Subgroups of an Arbitrary Group

Let G be any group.

  1. If H and K are subgroups of G, show that H∩K is a subgroup of G.

    Previously done here.

  2. Let H and K are subgroups of G. Prove that if H⊂K, then H is a subgroup of K.

    Since H is a subgroup of G, it is closed under multiplication and inversion. This combined with the fact that it is a subset of K, makes it a subgroup of K.

  3. The center of a group is the set of all its elements that commute with all elements of the group. C = {a∈G: xa=ax ∀x∈G}. Prove that C is a subgroup of G.

    Let p,q∈C. For any x∈G, xp=px and xq=qx. Therefore, pqx=pxq=xpq. Since pq commutes with any element of G, pq∈C, and C is closed under multiplication.

    Let p∈C and x∈G.

    pp-1x inverse

    Since pxp-1=pp-1, x and p-1 commute, and p-1∈C.

    Since C is closed under mulitplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  5. Let G be a finite group, and let S≠∅ be a subset of G that is closed under multiplication. Show that S is a subgroup of G.

    Since S⊆G, S is finite. Let |S|=n. Pick an element s∈S. Since S is closed under multiplication, T={s1, s2, ... sn, sn+1}⊆S. But, since |S|=n, at least two elements of T must be identical. Call two identical elements of T si and sj, with j>i. Then, sisj-i=sj=si. Therefore, sj-i=e∈S.

    For any s∈S, ∃k such that k>0 and sk=e. (In the first part of this exercise, k=j-i). s1sk-1=sk=e, so sk-1=s-1, and S is closed under inversion.

    Since S is closed under addition and negation, it is a subgroup of G. (Note that the only use made of the fact that G is finite was to establish that S is finite. Therefore, this result applies to a finite subset of any group, whether the group is finite or not.)

  6. If f:G→G, a is a period of f if f(ax)=f(x) ∀:x∈G. Prove that B={b∈G: b is a period of G} is a subgroup of G.

    Let p,q∈B. Then, for any x∈G, qx∈G. Therefore, f((pq)x)=f(p(qx))=f(x), and pq∈G. So, G is closed under multiplication.

    Let r∈B. Then, for any x∈G, rx∈G. f(r-1(rx))=f((r-1r)x)=f(ex)=f(x). Therefore, r-1∈B and B is closed under inversion.

    Since B is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  7. Let H be a subgroup of G and let K = {x∈G: xax-1∈H iff a∈H}. Prove:

    1. K is a subgroup of G:

      Let p,q∈K⊆G. Then, pap-1∈G, iff a∈H, as is qaq-1. To see if K is closed under multiplication, we check to see if pq∈K. Let qaq-1 = b. (pq)a(pq)-1 = pqaq-1p-1 = pbp-1 = c. Since p∈K, c∈H iff b is. Since q∈K, b∈H iff a is. So, pq satisfies the requirement for membership in K, and K is closed under multiplication.

      Let p∈K. Choose an element a∈G, and let b=pap-1. Then, b∈H iff a is. a = eae = (p-1p)a(p-1p) = p-1(pap-1)p = p-1bp, which is in H iff a is (since they are equal). Therefore, K is closed under inversion.

      Since K is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

    2. H is a subgroup of K:

      Let b∈H. Since H is a group, it is closed under inversion, and b-1∈H. Since a group is closed under multiplication, bab-1∈H whenever a is. Therefore, b∈H→b∈H, and H⊆K.

  8. Let G and H be groups, and let G×H be their direct product. Prove:

    1. L={(x,e): x∈G} is a subgroup of G×H:

      Let a,b∈G. Then, (a,e) and (b,e) are in G×H and in L. (a,e)(b,e)=(ab,e). Since ab∈G and e∈H, (ab,e)∈L, and L is closed under multiplication.

      Let c∈G. Then, (c,e)∈L. Since c-1∈G, (c-1,e)∈L, and (c,e)(c-1,e)=(e,e), L is closed under inversion.

      Because L is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup.

    2. M={(x,x): x∈G} is a subgroup of G×G:

      Let a,b∈G. Then, (a,a) and (b,b) are in G×G and in M. (a,a)(b,b)=(ab,ab). Since G is a group, it is closed under multiplication, and ab∈G. Therefore, (ab,ab)∈M, and M is closed under multiplication.

      Let c∈G. Then, (c,c)∈M. Since G is a group, it is closed under inversion. This means that c-1∈G, and that (c-1,c-1)∈M. Since (c,c)(c-1,c-1)=(e,e), M is closed under inversion

      Since M is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup.
