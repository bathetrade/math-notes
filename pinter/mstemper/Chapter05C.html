Problem C — Subgroups of Abelian Groups

In the following exercises, G is an Abelian group.

  1. Let H = {x∈G: x=x-1}. Show that H is a subgroup of G.

    Let a,b∈H. Then a=a-1 and b=b-1. Then, (ab)-1 = b-1a-1 = ba = ab, since elements are their own inverses and G is Abelian. Since ab is its own inverse, ab∈H and H is closed under multiplication.

    Let a∈H. Then a-1=a and therefore a-1∈H and H is closed under inversion.

    Since H is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  2. Note that the previous exercise is a special case of this one, with n=2, since x2=xx=xx-1=e.

    Let n∈Z be fixed, then let H = {x∈G: xn=e}. Show that H is a subgroup of G.

    Let a,b∈H. Then an=e and bn=e. Then, since G is Abelian, (ab)n = anbn = ee = e, and ab∈H, and H is closed under multiplication.

    Let a∈H. Then, (a-1)n = (an)-1 = e-1 = e, so a-1∈H, and H is closed under inversion.

    Since H is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  3. Let H = {x∈G: ∃y∈G such that y2=x}. Show that H is a subgroup of G.

    Let r,s∈G and let a=r2 and b=s2. Then, a,b∈H. Then, ab = r2s2 = (rs)2, since G is Abelian. Therefore, ab∈H and H is closed under multiplication.

    Let r∈G and let a=r2. Then, a-1 = (r2)-1 = (r-1)2, and a-1∈ H and H is closed under inversion.

    Since H is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  4. Let H be a subgroup of G, and let K = {x∈G: x2∈H}. Show that K is a subgroup of G.

    Let r,s∈G such that r2,s2∈H. Then r,s∈K. Since H is a group, it is closed under multiplication, so r2s2∈H. Since G is Abelian, r2s2 = (rs)2. So (rs)2∈H. Therefore, rs∈K and K is closed under multiplication.

    Let r∈G such that r2∈H. Since H is a group, it is closed under inversion, so is (r2)-1. Since (r2)-1=(r-1)2, r-1∈K and therefore, K is closed under inversion.

    Since K is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  5. Let H be a subgroup of G, and for some n∈Z (n>0), let K = {x∈G: xn∈H}. Show that K is a subgroup of G.

    The same proof as in the previous exercise, with n taking the place of 2, will work, since no special properties of 2 were used in that proof. In fact, the restriction that n>0 is not even needed. Since H is closed under inversion, -n will give the same set as n, and n=0 would give K=G.

  6. Let H and K be subgroups of G. Define HK = {xy: x∈H and y∈K}. Show that HK is a subgroup of G.

    Let a,b∈H and c,d∈K. Since H and K are groups, ab∈H and cd∈K. Therefore, (ab)(cd)∈HK. Since G is Abelian, (ab)(cd)=(ac)(bd), and (ac)(bd)∈HK. HK is therefore closed under multiplication.

    Let a∈H and c∈K. Since H and K are groups, a-1∈H and c-1∈K. Therefore, a-1c-1 = (ca)-1 = (ac)-1∈HK, and HK is closed under inversion.

    Since HK is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of G.

  7. Explain why exercises 4, 5, and 6 are not true if G is non-Abelian.

    In 4, we made use of the fact that r2s2 = (rs)2, which is only true when r and s commute. The same fact (with 2 replaced by n) was used in 5.

    In 6, commutativity was used to prove closure under both multiplication and inversion.
