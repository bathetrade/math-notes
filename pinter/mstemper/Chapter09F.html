Problem F — Isomorphism of Groups Given by Generators and Defining Equations

We are supposed to prove that each of the succeeding pairs of groups, G and G', are isomorphic.

In Chapter 5, Dr. Pinter brushed over the possibility of different groups (of different cardinalities) being generated by the same set of defining equations. For instance, in Problem F, Exercise 1, we need to consider the possibility that there is another combination of elements besides {e, a, b, b2, ab, ab2} that satisfies the equations a2=e, b3=e, ba=ab2. Could there be a different, possibly larger, set that meets these criteria?

This turns out not to be possible. Since G is a group that contains a and b, the closure property assures us that it also contains all possible products of any finite number of a's and b's. Any proposed additional element will turn out to be equivalent to one of the existing elements.

For instance, we could attempt to augment G with the element b2a. However, this turns out to be equivalent to ab, as may be seen in the Cayley table of G.

We may view the explicitly-stated members of a group defined by generators and defining equations as representatives of equivalence classes of all possible equivalent products. Practically speaking, it is not necessary to even specify the members of the group — the generators and equations uniquely determine the group. (This is implied by the title of Problem F of Chapter 5.)

With this issue resolved, I can now proceed to the exercises. All that is necessary to show isomorphism in each case is to show that the groups not specified by generating equations have elements that satisfy those equations. Preservation of all other products, as well as bijection, follow from that fact.

  1. G' is the dihedral group, D4.

    G is the subgroup of S4 generated by (24) and (1234); G' = {e, a, b, b2, b3, ab, ab2, ab3}, where a2=e, b4=e, ba=ab3.

    Since raising a cycle of length n to the nth power produces the identity, (24) ≘ a and (1234) ≘ b satisfy the first two equations.

    Substituting (24) for a and (1234) for b gives ba = (1234)(24) = (12)(34). Similar substitution gives ab3 = (24)(1234)(1234)(1234) = (12)(34).

    Since the proposed mapping satisfies all of the defining equations, these two groups are isomorphic.

  2. G = S3; G' = {e, a, b, ab, aba, abab}, where a2 = e, b2 = e, and bab = aba.

    If we let (12) ≘ a and (23) ≘ b, a2 = e and b2 = e are immediately satisfied.

    Substituting (12) for a and (23) for b gives aba = (12)(23)(12) = (13), and also gives bab = (23)(12)(23) = (13). Therefore, the third defining equation is also satisfied.

    Since the proposed mapping satisfies all of the defining equations, these two groups are isomorphic.

  3. To reduce ambiguity, I will use "A" and "B" to refer to the generators of D4 in this exercise.

    G = D4; G' = {e, a, b, ab, aba, (ab)2, ba, bab} where a2 = b2 = e, and (ab)4 = e.

    Mapping B ≘ (ab) satisfies the last equation. Finding two other elements in D4 that are self-inverses and whose product is B will satisfy the first two equations. Inspection of the Cayley table of D4 shows that A ≘ a and AB ≘ b meet the requirements.

    The final mapping is:

    G (D4) A B AB
    G' a ab b

    Since the proposed mapping satisfies all of the defining equations, these two groups are isomorphic.

  4. G = Z2 × Z2 × Z2; G' = {e, a, b, c, ab, bc, ac, abc} where a2 = b2 = c2 = e and (ab)2 = (bc)2 = (ac)2 = e.

    Map (1,0,0) ≘ a, (0,1,0) ≘ b, and (0,0,1) ≘ c. Since each element of Z2 is a self-inverse this mapping satisfies all of the defining equations, and these two groups are isomorphic.
