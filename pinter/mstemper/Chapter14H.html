Problem H — Inner Direct Products

If G is any group, let H and K be normal subgroups of G such that H ∩ K = {e}. Prove the following:

  1. Let {h1,h2} ⊆ H and {k1,k2} ⊆ K. Then, h1k1 = h2k2 implies h1 = h2 and k1 = k2.

    h1k1 h2k2 given
    h1-1h1k1 h1-1h2k2 premultiply both sides by h1-1
    ek1 h1-1h2k2 multiplicative inverse
    k1 h1-1h2k2 multiplicative identity
    k1k2-1 h1-1h2k2k2-1 postmultiply both sides by k2-1
    k1k2-1 h1-1h2e multiplicative inverse
    k1k2-1 h1-1h2 multiplicative identity

    Since K is a subgroup, it is closed under multiplication and inversion, so k1k2-1 ∈ K. Similarly, h1h2-1 ∈ H. Since both quantities are equal, they must both be in H ∩ K. Since H ∩ K = {e}, we have k1k2-1 = e = h1-1h2.

    Since k1k2-1 = e, k1 = k2. Similarly, since h1-1h2 = e, h2 = h1. ∎

  2. ∀ h ∈ H and ∀ k ∈ K, hk = kh.

    Since H is normal, k-1h-1k ∈ H. Since H is closed, (k-1h-1k)h ∈ H. Since K is normal, h-1kh ∈ K. Since K is closed, k-1(h-1kh) ∈ K.

    Due to associativity, (k-1h-1k)h = k-1(h-1kh). Thus, it/they ∈ H ∩ K = {e}.

  3. With the additional assumption that G = HK, show that φ(h,k) = hk is an isomorphism φ: H × K → G.

    That the domain of φ is H × K is obvious. That its codomain is G is likewise obvious. That it is onto H follows from the assumption that that G = HK.

    To see that φ is a homomorphism, all that is necessary is to show that it preserves products. Let {h1,h2} ⊆ H and {k1,k2} ⊆ K. Then:

    φ(h1,k1) φ(h2,k2) (h1k1)(h2k2) definition of φ
    h1(k1h2)k2 associativity
    h1(h2k1)k2 exercise 2
    (h1h2)(k1k2 associativity
    φ(h1h2,k1k2) definition of φ

    Therefore, φ preserves products and is a homomorphism. By Exercise 1 it is one-to-one, making is a bijection.

    Since φ is a bijective homomorphism, it is an isomorphism. ∎
