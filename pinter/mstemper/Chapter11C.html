Problem C — Generators of Cyclic Groups

Let φ(j) be the number of positive integers less than j and relatively prime to j. Let ord(a) = n, and prove the following:

  1. ar is a generator of <a> iff r and n are relatively prime.

    If (ar)p = arp = e only when rp is a multiple of n. If r and n are relatively prime, then lcm(r,n) = rn. This means that there are n distinct powers of ar, and ar generates <a>.

    Only If If ar generates <a>, there are n distinct powers of ar. If gcd(r,n) >1, then lcm(r,n) = rn/gcd(r,n) < rn, which would mean that there were fewer than n distinct powers of ar, and ar could not generate all n elements of <a>.

  2. <a> has φ(n) different generators.

    As shown in the previous exercise, ar is a generator of <a> iff n and r are relatively prime. By the Division Theorem, we can ignore any r≥n, since each corresponds to some 0≤r<n. We can also ignore r=0, since ar = e does not generate any non-trivial groups.

    We are left with integers in the range 1≤r<n to consider. Since ar is a generator of <a> iff r is relatively prime to n, we end up with φ(n) different generators.

  3. For any m|n, let Cm = {x∈<a> : xm = e}. Cm is a subgroup of <a>.

    A simpler proof, for the more general case of Abelian groups and any exponent, was done in Chapter 5, Problem C, Exercise 2.

    Select any two elements of Cm, ar and as. By the definition of Cm, arm = e = asm. aras = ar+s.

    (ar+s)m a(r+s)m laws of exponents
      arm+sm distributive property
      armasm laws of exponents
      (ar)m(as)m laws of exponents
      ee arm = e = asm

    Since (ar+s)m = e, aras ∈ Cm, and Cm is closed under multiplication.

    Let ap ∈ Cm.

    (a-p)m a-pm laws of exponents
      a-mp commutativity of multiplication
      (a-m)p laws of exponents
      (ea-m)p identity
      (ana-m)p ord(<a>) = n
      (akma-m)p m|n
      (akm-m)p laws of exponents
      (a(k-1)m)p distributive property
      a(k-1)mp laws of exponents
      a(k-1)pm commutativity of multiplication
      (a(k-1)p)m laws of exponents
      (ap(k-1))m commutativity of multiplication
      ((ap)(k-1))m laws of exponents
      (e(k-1))m ap ∈ Cm
      e identity

    Therefore, if ap ∈ Cm, then a-p ∈ Cm.

    Since Cm is a subset of <a> that closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup of <a>.

  4. Cm has exactly m elements.

    Let k = n/m. Then, (aik)m = aikm = (akm)i = ei = e. This means that all members of {aik : i∈Z} are members of Cm. This set has m unique members: ajk (1≤j≤m).

    Suppose that ap ∉ {aik : i∈Z}. In this case, p = jk+x, where 0<x<k, by the Division Algorithm.

    (ap)m = (ajk+x)m = ajkm+xm = (akm)jaxm = ejaxm = axm. Since 0<x<k, xm is not a multiple of n, and (ap)m ≠ e.

    Therefore Cm = {aik : i∈Z} and has exactly m elements.

  5. An element x in <a> has order m iff x is a generator of Cm.

    If If x is a generator of Cm, then we have the facts that xm = e and |{xi : i∈Zm}| = |Cm| = m. Therefore, m is the smallest positive integer such that xm = e, and hence is the order of x.

    Only If If ord(x) = m, then xm = e, and |{xi : i∈Zm}| = m. Therefore, if all of the elements in |{xi : i∈Zm}| are in Cm, x generates Cm.

    Let j∈Zm. (xj)m = xjm = (xm)j = ej = e. Therefore, xj∈Cm, and x is a generator of Cm.

  6. There are φ(m) elements of order m in <a>.

    By Exercise 5, the elements in <a> that are of order m are also the generators of Cm.

    By Exercise 2, a cyclic group of order m has φ(m) different generators. Cm is a cyclic group. By Exercise 4, Cm is of order m.

    Therefore, Cm has φ(m) generators, which are the elements of <a> with order m.

  7. Let n = mk. Then ar has order m iff r = kl, where l and m are relatively prime.

    Restating it this way covers both "If" and "Only If".

    The given condition may be rewritten if we observe that the requirement that l and m be relatively prime implies that k = gcd(n,r). Then, m = n/gcd(n,r). We can then restate the conjecture as ord(ar) = n/gcd(n,r) = lcm(n,r)/r.

    This was shown to be true in Exercise G5 of Chapter 10.

  8. If c is any generator of <a>, then { cr : r is relatively prime to n } is the set of all generators of <a>.

    If c is a generator of <a>, then ord(c) = n (= |<a>|). By Theorem 5 of Chapter 10, ct = e iff t is a multiple of n. Therefore, cpr = e iff pr is a multiple of n. Since gcd(r,n) = 1 (r and n are relatively prime), lcm(r,n) = rn, and cr has n distinct powers, which makes it a generator of <a>.

    Since there are φ(n) positive integers less than n that are relatively prime to it, and <a> has φ(n) generators (as shown in Exercise 2), { cr : r is relatively prime to n } must contain all of the generators of <a>.
