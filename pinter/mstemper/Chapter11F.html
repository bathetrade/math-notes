  2. Prove that if m is a multiple of gcd(k,n), am has a k-th root in ⟨a⟩.

    Since n = ord(a), anp = (an)p = ep = e. Therefore, anp+m = am.

    If (np+m)/k is an integer, then (a(np+m)/k)k = anp+m = am, which makes a(np+m)/k a k-th root of am.

    Let (np+m)/k = q. Then np+m = qk, or np-kq = m. Since m was specified to be a multiple of gcd(k,n), it can be expressed as a linear combination of k and n, which means that q is an integer. Since q = (np+m)/k, aq is a k-th root of am.

  3. Prove that if am has a k-th root in ⟨a⟩, m is a multiple of gcd(k,n).

    Is it valid to derive xk = np+m from axk = anp+m?

    Let ax be the k-th root of am. Then, (ax)k = am = e∗am = epam = (an)pam = anp+m for some p.

    Then xk = np+m, or m = xk-np. Therefore, m can be expressed as a linear combination of n and k. A simple fact of number theory shows that this means m must be a multiple of gcd(k,n).

  4. Prove that a has a k-th root in ⟨a⟩ iff k and n are relatively prime.

    Remembering that a = a1, we see that (by the two previous exercises) a1 has a k-th root in ⟨a⟩ iff 1 is a multiple of gcd(k,n). This is only true when gcd(k,n) = 1; in other words, when k and n are relatively prime.

  5. Let p be a prime number. Prove that:

    1. If n is not a multiple of p, then every element in ⟨a⟩ has a p-th root.

      If p is prime and n is not a multiple of p, then gcd(n,p) = 1, and every positive integer m is a multiple of gcd(n,p). Therefore, by Exercises 2 and 3, am has a p-th root.

    2. If n is a multiple of p, and am has a p-th root, then m is a multiple of p.

      For am to have a p-th root, m must be a multiple of gcd(p,n). If n is a multiple of a prime p, then gcd(n,p) = p. Therefore, m must be a multiple of p.

  6. Prove that the set of all elements of ⟨a⟩ having a k-th root is a subgroup of ⟨a⟩. Explain why this subgroup is cyclic, say ⟨am⟩. What is the value of m?

    An element of ⟨a⟩ has a k-th root is of the form aq∗gcd(n,k). Furthermore, every element of this form has a k-th root.

    Let ai∗gcd(n,k) and aj∗gcd(n,k) be two such elements. They may also be written as (agcd(n,k))i and (agcd(n,k))j. Their product is and (agcd(n,k))i(agcd(n,k))j = (agcd(n,k))(i+j) = a(i+j)∗gcd(n,k). Therefore, this set is closed under multiplication.

    For ai∗gcd(n,k) in this set, ai∗gcd(n,k)a(-i)∗gcd(n,k) = a(i+(-i))∗gcd(n,k) = a0∗gcd(n,k) = a0 = e. Therefore, this set is closed under multiplication.

    Since this set is closed under multiplication and inversion, it is a subgroup.

    Since all members of this set are of the form aq∗gcd(n,k), it is a cyclic group with generator agcd(n,k).
