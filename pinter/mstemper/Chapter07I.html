Problem I — The Algebra of Kinship Structures

Let K be the set of clans in a society, with ki∈K for i∈{1, ... n}. We have the following rules:

  1. The clans form an equivalence relation on the society.
  2. If a man is a member of kj, then he must marry a woman from clan w(kj), where w:K→K.
  3. If w(x)=w(y) then x=y.
  4. If a man is a member of kj, then his children are in clan c(kj), where c:K→K.
  5. If c(x)=c(y) then x=y.
  6. A man cannot marry a woman of his own clan: w(x)≠x.
  7. For any x,y there is a permutation α that is the composite of various of w, w-1, c, and c-1 such that α(x)=y.
  8. If for any α and β that are compostions of the various kinship relations α(kj) = β(kj), then α(ki) = β(ki) for all i∈{1, ... n}

Letting G be the group of all compostions of the various kinship relations and their inverses, prove items 1-3, and explain items 4-9:

  1. If α(ki)=ki for any clan ki, then α=e (the identity).

    e(kj)=kj for any clan kj. Since α(ki)=ki, Rule viii says that α = e.

  2. ∃m≤n such that αm = e.

    Since |K|=n, ∃ p,q (1≤p,q≤n+1 and p≠q) such that αp(ki) = αq(ki), by the pigeon-hole principle. Without loss of generality, let p>q. Rule viii implies that αp-q=e. Since p≤n+1, p-q≤n.

  3. G consists of exactly n permutations.

    By rule vii, there must be at least n permutations, in order to map x to each different ki∈K. If there were more than n permutations, two of them would map x to the same ki. By rule viii, these two permutations would be identical. Therefore, there are exactly n permutations.

  4. If a person belongs to clan ki, their father belongs to c-1(ki). If a woman belongs to clan kj, her husband belongs to w-1(kj).

    The identity function maps a clan to itself. Since all children of a father belong to the same clan, the clan of the children of the father of a person is that person's clan, which is modeled by c∘c-1 = e. Since the wives of all of the men in a given clan are from one (different) clan, the clan of the wives of the men in the clan of one woman's husband is that person's clan, which is modeled by w∘w-1 = e.

  5. If a man is in the same clan as his son, c=e. If a woman is in the same clan as her son, c=w.

    If a man and his son are both in clan kj, then c(kj) = kj. By Exercise 1, c=e.

    If a man is in clan kj, then his wife is in clan w(kj) and his son (hence her son) is in clan c(kj). If w(kj) = c(kj), then w=c by rule viii.

  6. If a person is in clan ki, the clan of their mother's sister's son is c∘w-1∘w∘c-1(ki), which prohibits marriage between matrilineal parallel cousins.

    Call the person in question "x". Since x is in clan ki, the father of x is in clan c-1(ki), the mother of x is in clan w∘c-1(ki), as is her sister. Then, the husband of x's mother's sister is in clan w-1∘w∘c-1(ki), and the son of x's mother's sister's husband (who is, of course, also the son of x's mother's sister) is in clan c∘w-1∘w∘c-1(ki).

    By rule ii, people from the same clan cannot marry.

  7. Marriage between a man and the daughter of his father's sister is prohibited.

    Let the man be "y", and let him be a member of clan kj. The father of y is a member of c-1(kj), as is the sister of the father of y. The husband of the sister of the father of y comes from clan w-1∘c-1(kj), and his daughter is a member of clan c∘w-1∘c-1(kj). If a man can marry the daughter of his father's sister, then w = c∘w-1∘c-1.
