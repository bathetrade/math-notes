Problem C — Elementary Consequences of Lagrange's Theorem

Let G be a finite group. Prove or disprove each of the following:

  1. If |G| = n, then xn = e ∀ x ∈ G.

    Let |⟨x⟩| = m. By Lagrange's Theorem, m = |⟨x⟩| | |G| = n. Let km = n. By Theorem 5 of Chapter 10, xkm = xn = e.

  2. Let |G| = pq, where p and q are primes. Then either G is cyclic or every element x ∈ G, x≠e has order p or q.

    I'm less than clear on why Pinter phrased this in a way that implies that these are mutually exclusive. In fact, Z3 × Z5 ≘ Z15.

    Let x ∈ G. Then, by the previous exercise, xpq = e. Therefore, ord(x) ∈ {1, p, q, pq}. Discarding x=e, we have ord(x) ∈ {p, q, pq}.

    If ∃ y ∈ G such that ord(y) = pq, then G = ⟨y⟩ and is therefore cyclic. If ∄ y ∈ G such that ord(y) = pq, then ord(y) ∈ {p, q} ∀ y≠e.

  3. Let |G| = 4. Either G is cyclic, or every member of G is its own inverse. Conclude from this that every group of order 4 is Abelian.

    By the previous exercise, either G is cyclic, or every element other than the identity has order 2. In other words, either the group is cyclic or every element is its own inverse, since aa=e implies a = a-1.

    All cyclic groups are Abelian. On the other hand, if every element is its own inverse:

    ab c given
    b ac pre-multiply by a, which is its own inverse
    bc a post-multiply by c, which is its own inverse
    c ba pre-multiply by b, which is its own inverse

    Therefore, ab=ba, and the group is Abelian.

  4. If G has an element of order p and an element of order q, where p and q are distinct primes, then the order of G is a multiple of pq.

    Let ord(a) = p and let ord(b) = q. Since p≠q, we know a≠b. |⟨a⟩| = p and |⟨b⟩| = q. Since ⟨a⟩ and ⟨b⟩ are subgroups of G, p and q both divide |G|. Therefore, |G| is a multiple of both p and q. Since they're prime, they're relatively prime, which means that a mulitple of p and q is a multiple of pq.

  5. If G has an element of order k and an element of order m, then the order of G is a multiple of lcm(k,m).

    Let ord(a) = k and let ord(b) = m. |⟨a⟩| = k and |⟨b⟩| = m. Since ⟨a⟩ and ⟨b⟩ are subgroups of G, k and m both divide |G|. Therefore, |G| is a multiple of both p and q, which makes it a mulitple of lcm(p,q).

  6. Let p be a prime number. In any finite group, the number of elements of order p is a multiple of p-1.

    If an element is of order p, then it's a member of a cyclic subgroup of order p. That subgroup has p elements, one of which is the identity element. Discarding the identity, which has order 1, leaves p-1 elements of that subgroup having order p.

    If there are multiple subgroups of order p, they are disjoint except for the identity element. Therefore, if there are m subgroups of order p, there are m·(p-1) elements of order p.

    If no elements have order p, then there are 0·(p-1) elements of order p.
